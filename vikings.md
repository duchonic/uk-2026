# Vikings in England

## First Raids

The earliest recorded Viking raids on England took place along the coast:

- **786–802 — Portland, Dorset**: Three ships arrived on the Dorset coast, marking the first known Viking contact with West Saxon territory. A royal official who rode out to meet them was killed.
- **793 — Lindisfarne**: The raid on the monastery of Holy Island shocked the Christian world. Monks were killed, treasures looted, and others taken as slaves. It is widely seen as the start of the Viking Age in Britain.
- **794 — Northumbria**: A further raid struck the coast of Northumbria, including the monastery of Jarrow, consolidating the pattern of coastal attacks on monastic wealth.

## Northumbria

Northumbria was one of the great Anglo-Saxon kingdoms, covering roughly modern-day northeast England and southeast Scotland — the entire stretch of our trip from Dunbar down to York. It was formed from the earlier kingdoms of Bernicia (north) and Deira (south), and by the 8th century was a major centre of Christian learning, home to figures like the Venerable Bede at Jarrow and the monks of Lindisfarne.

It was precisely this wealth and piety that made Northumbria the first sustained target of Viking raids. After the initial attacks on Lindisfarne (793) and Jarrow (794), the raids intensified over the following decades. In **866**, the **Great Heathen Army** — a large coalition of Danish warriors — crossed into Northumbria and captured York, which they renamed **Jorvik**. Northumbria became a Viking kingdom, with York as its capital.

The Vikings ruled Northumbria on and off for nearly a century. The last Viking king of York, **Eric Bloodaxe**, was killed at the Battle of Stainmore in **954**, bringing the Viking kingdom to an end and incorporating Northumbria into the emerging Kingdom of England.

The legacy is deep: many place names across the region ending in *-by* (Whitby, Selby, Grimsby), *-thorpe*, or *-thwaite* are Norse in origin, and the Danelaw — the legal and cultural zone under Viking influence — covered most of this area for generations.

## On Our Route: Edinburgh to London

Several places on our trip fall directly in Viking territory.

### Lindisfarne (Holy Island) — between Dunbar and Newcastle
The site of the famous 793 raid. Holy Island is about 60 miles south of Dunbar and 50 miles north of Newcastle — a natural stop on our drive down on 28 July. The ruins of Lindisfarne Priory still stand, and the tidal causeway to the island adds to the atmosphere.

### Whitby — 31 July to 2 August
The name "Whitby" is itself Norse (*hvítr* = white, *bý* = settlement). The Vikings raided and eventually settled here. Whitby Abbey was sacked during the raids. The town later became part of the Danelaw — the large swath of northern and eastern England under Viking rule from the late 9th century.

### York (Jorvik) — 3 August
York was captured by the Great Heathen Army in 866 and renamed **Jorvik**. It became the capital of a Viking kingdom and one of the most important Norse cities in Europe. The **Jorvik Viking Centre** (Coppergate) reconstructs life in Viking-age York and is well worth a visit.

### Newcastle / Jarrow — 28–30 July
Jarrow, just east of Newcastle, was the site of the 794 raid. The monastery of St Paul's at Jarrow (where the Venerable Bede lived and worked) was attacked. The church still exists and is one of the oldest in England.

## Yorkshire

Yorkshire is the largest historic county in England, covering a big stretch of northern England south of Northumbria. The name itself is Viking in origin: it derives from **Jorvik-shire** — the shire (administrative district) centred on the Viking city of York. So Yorkshire is, in a sense, a direct political legacy of the Norse occupation.

After the Norman Conquest (1066), it was divided into three administrative "Ridings" — from Old Norse *þriðjungr*, meaning "third part": North, East, and West Riding. In 1974 it was split into smaller modern counties (North Yorkshire, West Yorkshire, South Yorkshire, East Riding), but "Yorkshire" remains a powerful cultural identity.

The Norse legacy is visible across the landscape in place names ending in *-by*, *-thorpe*, and *-thwaite* — all Old Norse in origin. Whitby, Selby, Grimsby, and dozens of villages across the region bear this stamp.

**On our trip**, we pass through North Yorkshire the whole way down from Newcastle:
- **Whitby** — Norse name, Viking settlement, on the moors coast
- **York** — the Viking capital Jorvik itself
- **Knaresborough and Harrogate** — inland North Yorkshire, spa and market towns
